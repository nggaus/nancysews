Hello to my friends in central New York

As you have likely heard, there is a national shortage of surgical masks. Hospitals are in desperate need. They are beginning to take hand sewn masks as a stop gap until the shortages are alleviated.

There are plenty of patterns and advice online, and a lot of people jumping in to make masks, but it wasn’t clear to me that there was any organized effort for central New York (maybe, but I haven’t been able to find any). I wasn’t willing to proceed until I knew that 1) what I was making was acceptable; 2) I knew where they were needed locally; and 3) I knew how to deliver them without creating more confusion at busy hospitals and medical facilities.

I am finally getting answers. As of this writing, SUNY Upstate is beginning to take donations from people who are willing and able to make masks. There is a hospital committee that has looked into the materials and design needed to make them as safe as possible for their healthcare workers to use. I have posted their information below.

Questions that you probably have that I think I have answers for:

  • How do I know I have the right materials in my stash?
    Current instructions are calling for tightly woven, prewashed cotton (quilting cotton will do), prewashed flannel if you have it (for filling), either narrow flat elastic or elastic cord OR self fabric ties (ties are probably better because they are adjustable), and, if you are making shaped masks, you will need a nose piece (see below).

  • I don’t have the materials at home. Where can I get them?
    You can purchase locally from stores listed below or order online. JoAnn Fabrics is now offering curb-side service if you order ahead.

  • I know how to sew but I don’t have a machine. Where can I get one?
    Ask friends and neighbors if they have any old machines in their closets. As long as it can sew a solid straight stitch, you’re good. If you can’t get your hands on one, then pair up with someone who does have one and offer to cut out fabric pieces or make a run to the fabric store for supplies (for front porch drop offs). JoAnn Fabrics is now offering curb-side service if you order ahead.

    If you are really at a loss, you can sew the masks by hand. Make sure they are sturdy enough to stand up to repeated washing though.

  • What design should I use and are there instructions?
    There are many designs out there. SUNY Upstate has released their instruction sheet for a close fitting, shaped mask which they prefer. I have an updated version of these instructions (uses the same pattern pieces) that will work with a flat aluminum nose piece. You may want to take a look at the updated version even if you are using pipe cleaners, heavy twist ties, or floral wire for your nose piece since this alternate assembly method might be more to your liking. SUNY Upstate will be distributing any extra of these sewn masks to other hospitals in Syracuse.

    If you are a "sloppy sewer" (hey, I don't judge!) then go for the rectangular mask since it's easier to sew. But if you have some reasonable sewing chops, try the shaped mask since it is what the hospitals prefer. They are going to need lots of them!

    Beth Eischen Call has these great instructions for the rectangular masks she is making for distribution to medical labs and other healthcare facilities. If you want to add a wire or metal strip to a rectangular mask to make a better fit, see this information sheet with tips on adding a casing to the top edge of your masks.

    Some people are reporting that the elastic ear straps are too loose on some people. For this reason, I am starting to make fabric straps that tie behead the head instead. If anyone knows for sure that ear loops are preferred over tying behind the head, please let me know. Regardless, fabric straps (in lieu of elastic) are now being requested by many facilities.

    WeNeedMasks.org has information at the national level. Their designs may be suitable for healthcare facilities other than SUNY Upstate, but check first before you sew.

  • Where do I take them when I have a bunch made?
    We need to minimize traffic at the hospitals. Some collection points are listed below.

  • But cloth masks are not sterile. Can they really be used?
    At this point, due to extreme shortages, the CDC is loosening it's guidelines and allowing healthcare centers to use cloth masks. It's better than going without. In some situations, the cloth masks can be used over top of the approved sterile masks to extend their lifespan. In other situations, the cloth masks will be the only thing available. Be sure to prewash your fabrics before making the masks, thoroughly wash your hands before working on them, clean your sewing area, and pack completed masks in plastic bags or boxes for delivery. Healthcare centers will probably be washing them before use. If you are sick or have potentially been exposed to someone who is sick, please wait until you are recovered and out of danger before sewing masks!!

  • Nose pieces: There are a number of things you can use for the nose pieces on the shaped masks. Some people are using pipe cleaners or heavy twist ties (unused). Floral wire has also been suggested as a viable alternative.

    ✶ You can use metal strips cut from aluminum roof flashing -- Cut 1/4" strips and trim them to 5" lengths. You can use either a pair of metal snips or heavy scissors. Nip off the corners so they are more "user friendly" and slide into the casing easier. The more accurate you make the ¼” cut the better -- these need to slide into a narrow casing and if they are too wide, they won’t fit!  If the edges seem sharp, you can sand them lightly, but I’ve found this to be unnecessary.
